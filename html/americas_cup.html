Introduction

The America's Cup is widely regarded as the “Formula 1 of the seas”, the pinnacle of international yacht racing. The competition began in 1851, when the Schooner America crossed the Atlantic to compete in a race around the Isle of Wight, organized by the Royal Yacht Squadron. The American yacht won decisively, and the trophy it claimed would eventually become known as the America's Cup. The event predates the modern Olympic Games (first held in 1896) and many of the world's most prestigious sporting competitions. Over more than 170 years, the America's Cup has evolved from a traditional sailing regatta into a high-technology, globally broadcast sporting spectacle that blends tradition, innovation, and national pride.

Historical Background

The inaugural race took place on August 22, 1851, around the Isle of Wight in England. The schooner America, representing the New York Yacht Club, defeated a fleet of British yachts. When Queen Victoria reportedly asked who finished second, the famous reply was: “Your Majesty, there is no second.” From 1851 until 1983, the New York Yacht Club held the trophy, the longest winning streak in sporting history. That dominance ended when Australia II, representing the Royal Perth Yacht Club, claimed victory in 1983, marking a turning point in the competition's global profile. Since then, the Cup has seen fierce rivalries among sailing powerhouses such as the United States, New Zealand, Italy, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom. Teams such as Emirates Team New Zealand and Oracle Team USA have shaped the modern era with groundbreaking technological advancements.

The Auld Mug

The prize of the competition is the Auld Mug, a silver trophy made in 1848 by Garrard & Co. In 1851, it was donated to be used as a prize for the annual regatta, which later became the America's Cup. The winning team keeps the mug until that team is challenged and the mug is won back.

The competition

Unlike most sporting events, the America's Cup operates on a “defender vs. challenger” model:

  • The defending yacht club sets the venue and works with a selected Challenger of Record to define the rules for the next edition.
  • Multiple challengers compete to earn the right to race the defender in the final series.
  • The winner of the final match becomes the new defender and hosts the next event.

This structure gives the defender significant influence over the event’s design, including boat class, location, and race format.

Rules and Eligibility

All challenges for the America's Cup are governed by the Deed of Gift, the legal document that defines the rules of competition. The Deed ensures the event remains a match race between yacht clubs rather than commercial entities. To issue a valid challenge:

  • The challenging club must be a formally recognized yacht club from a country different than the defender.
  • The challenging club must have previously held an annual regetta on ocean waters or an arm of the sea.
  • Challenges must propose race dates (at least 10 months prior) and detailed yacht specifications, adhering to strict rules.

The format of the competition has evolved over time. Today, challengers compete in elimination series (such as the Challenger Selection Series) to determine who will face the defending champion in the final match.

Technical and Engineering aspect

Modern America's Cup yachts are among the most advanced racing yachts ever built. Each competing yacht represents the cutting edge of:

  • Material Science: Use of carbon fiber composites and ultra-lightweight construction
  • Aerodynamics: Optimization of sails, wing elements, and airflow management.
  • Hydrodynamics: Hull design, foil shapes, and water resistance reduction.
  • Data Analytics: Real-time performance monitoring and simulation modeling.

Recent editions feature hydrofoiling yachts that “fly” above the water at speeds exceeding 50 knots (nearly 100 kmh). These boats rely on precision engineering and constant data analysis to remain stable and competitive.
